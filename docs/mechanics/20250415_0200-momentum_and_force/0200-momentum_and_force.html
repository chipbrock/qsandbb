2 Isaac Newton‚Äôs important idea

To start something moving from rest? Apply a force. To speed up or slow down something already moving? Apply a force! To cause something to deviate from a straight line? Yes. Another force. To keep something moving at a constant speed? No (net) force required, thank you.

Here‚Äôs what he said:

Important

ü´µ Whenever there‚Äôs a change of velocity on any object, a force is at work: since acceleration is a change in velocity, forces are responsible for acceleration. Note: because velocity is technically a vector, then changing direction is also changing velocity.

One of the many ways that Newton got into the textbooks was to argue with Aristotle who insisted that all motion requires a pusher in physical contact: No, Newton said. Constant motion is free. It‚Äôs only accelerated motion that requires payment in the form of the action of a force.

Let‚Äôs start slowly and sneak up on this idea. Impulsively.

2.1 Impulse ‚Äì Every sport

In QS&BB we don‚Äôt need much depth, but we do need three important concepts‚Ä¶the ones that underpinned Newton‚Äôs system: force, mass, and momentum. Let‚Äôs move:

In the previous lesson we laid out the rules that govern an object‚Äôs motion.

Newton: To get something up to speed, you need to whack it or shove it ‚Äî either a sharp collision or a steady push increases the speed of an object. Push harder? More speed. Push longer? Again, more speed.

So let‚Äôs codify that everyday notion into a model of forces and motion. Let‚Äôs imagine a force, \(F\) that pushes during some time interval, \(\Delta t\). A whack means that \(\Delta t\) is small (like a golf club hitting a ball) while a steady shove (like a rugby scrum) means that the force is slowly applied so \(\Delta t\) is larger.

Here‚Äôs what we know from experience: applying a force (\(F\)) to something for a time interval (\(\Delta t\)) results in the speed increasing in proportion, (\(\Delta v\)). We have the beginnings of a model:

\[ \begin{equation} F\Delta t \propto \Delta v. \label{eq:impulse1} \end{equation} \]

Increase \(F\), \(\Delta t\), or both on the left-hand side, and the speed goes up on the right-hand side. The quantity on the left side is called the Impulse. It‚Äôs the sports-quantity. Any game involving a ball involves impulse and exceptional athletes can control both the \(F\) and the \(\Delta t\). The quantity on the right implies that the speed changed and of course if the speed changed, then the object accelerated and/or it changed direction.

We need to refine the model. But first some units and language:

May the force be with you by so many different names

We will use forces many times in QS&BB, which is a common quantity in our lives because of ‚Äúweight.‚Äù But if you‚Äôre from the United States, when you step on the bathroom scale it reports back to you your weight in the Imperial measurement system (or ‚Äúcustomary measures system‚Äù) as too many Pounds, lbs. In a minute, you‚Äôll see why that‚Äôs confusing when comparing to the rest of the world where the bathroom scale would report kilograms.

The unit of force in the International System of Units (SI), which includes the older ‚Äúmetric system‚Äù or MKS (Meter, Kilogram, Second) is the Newton, N. If you go to the gas station in Berlin, you‚Äôll fill your tires to a pressure measured in Pascals, which is Newtons per square meter. Of course up the street from me, our Michigan gas station reports pounds per square inch for my pressure.

We‚Äôll predominantly use the unit of Newton as the modern measure of a force ‚Äì like the rest of the civilized world. I‚Äôll not obsess with units and so I‚Äôm happy for you to rely on Mr. Google in almost all instances.

3 Newton‚Äôs Mass

Mass is a toughy and we‚Äôll see over and over how complicated it is ‚Äì to the current day. Here‚Äôs how he defined it in the Principia:

‚ÄúMass is the quantity of matter arising from its density and bulk conjointly.‚Äù

There you go. Useful? ‚Ä¶no?

You do perfectly well to accept the idea that mass is the quantitative measure of an object‚Äôs reluctance to be coaxed into changing its motion.

Here begins our love-hate relationship with mass. This much works:

Important

ü´µ Inertia is an object‚Äôs resistance to being accelerated and the measure of an object‚Äôs inertia is its mass.

This is the most profound and at the same time, the most mundane idea that Newton had!

Tip

4 The ‚ÄúQuantity of Motion‚Äù: Momentum

We just developed a sense that our hand-built, car-pushing formula, Equation \(\eqref{eq:impulse1}\) has to depend on speed and mass and so we‚Äôll just add it in on the right-hand side to get:

\[ \begin{equation} F\Delta t = m\Delta v \label{impulse2} \end{equation} \]

Being more explicit, when fleshed out impulse is

\[ \begin{equation} F\Delta t = m(v - v_0) \label{fullimpulse} \end{equation} \]

There‚Äôs more to this story

To be totally correct, the right hand side of this equation can be written as \(\Delta (mv)\) which recognizes that the mass might change instead of, or in addition to, the velocity changing. This is how a balloon jumps from your hand when you stop pinching the nozzle, or how a rocket is propelled by ejecting burned propellent out the back. In each case, the mass of the moving object changes and that results in a thrust. But we‚Äôll not encounter that in QS&BB.

This quantity on the right, \(mv\) is the definition of momentum. So the impulse is: $$ \[\begin{align} F\Delta t &= \Delta (mv) \nonumber \\ F\Delta t &= \Delta p \label{momentumdef} \\ \end{align}\] $$

Equation \(\eqref{newtons2b}\) is the T-shirt version for motion in which the mass of an object undergoing a push doesn‚Äôt change.

4.1 Newton‚Äôs 3 laws of motion

If there‚Äôs a second law, there must be a first one and there is‚Ä¶and a third one. Here they are for completeness:

  1. An object with constant velociy will retain that constant velocity of zero or another value.
  2. Best expressed as an equation as \(\eqref{newtons2}\).
  3. For every force that object A applies to object B, an equal and opposite force will be appled by B to A.

The Third law is very useful for solving problems, but that‚Äôs not what we‚Äôre about in QS&BB.

4.2 Weight

There‚Äôs an important special case of Equation \(\eqref{newtons2b}\) which has to do with the earth and, well, everything that‚Äôs not nailed down. The earth attracts all objects with a force (which we‚Äôll talk about in a bit) that seems constant and has a partcular value of acceleration, that of gravity., We affectionately call it \(g\). So your weight is the force that the earth applies to you and so we simply have: \[ \begin{align} F &= ma \text{ ...now modify for gravitational force}\nonumber \\ F &= W \text{ for the special force called "weight"} \nonumber \\ a &= g \text{ for the special acceleration of gravity} \nonumber\\ W &=mg \text{ which is weight on earth} \label{weight} \end{align} \] Two caveats:

  1. We call this \(g\) ‚Äúlittle g‚Äù and it‚Äôs only got a specific value for the earth. There are different little g‚Äôs for other planets, moons, or stars.
  2. The force of gravity is actually not constant ‚Äì stay tuned. But miles above tthe earth it‚Äôs still so close to \(g\) that we often pretend that it‚Äôs constant.

So little g is an important number:

  • In the american system, \(g = 32\) feet/second\(^2\) , 32 ft/s\(^2\)
  • For the rest of the world, \(g = 9.8\) meters/second\(^2\), 9.8 m/s$2

5 Circular Motion

Remember that if a velocity changes, then there‚Äôs an force acting as a cause and an acceleration is the result. Well, what about driving around a curve keeping your accelerator at 50 mph? This is the one time in QS&BB we‚Äôll care about the vector nature of velocity. A true (for a professional physicist or student) velocity has a magnitude (what your speedometer reads back) and a direction. So going 50 mph north is a different velocity than going 50 mph east. Same speed, different velocity.

Okay, what‚Äôs going on with you turning that corner where your direction changes but your speed doesn‚Äôt. From the formal definition, the velocity must change üëâ so there must be a force acting üëâ so there is an acceleration.

It might be easier to think of twirling something overhead. Suppose you tie a ball to a thin rope and get it rotating above your head in a horizontal plane, like our little guy in the margin.

You can feel a tug. That is, the ball seems to exert a force on your hand through the rope. But what‚Äôs really going on is that the ball wants to go forward (Newton‚Äôs first law) and you pull it back incrementally, bit by bit as it goes around. You‚Äôre supplying that force necessary to defeat its straight line motion.

This force-towards-the-center is called ‚Äúcentripetal force‚Äù ‚Äì you‚Äôve maybe heard of ‚Äúcentrifugal force‚Äù but that‚Äôs different and sometimes called ‚Äúficticious‚Äù which seems sort of insulting. But when you‚Äôre a passenger in a car that‚Äôs going around a curve, you feel a force that‚Äôs away from the center of the curve‚Ä¶that‚Äôs centifugal. But that‚Äôs just your body trying to obey that first law and go straight, while you‚Äôre in this car going, well, not straight. Where do you get the force that makes you follow the car? The door against your shoulder, the friction of the back of your lap on the seat, and hopefully your seatbelt. Absent those forces which pull you toward the center of the curve, you‚Äôd go flying.

The acceleration that you (or the ball) experience is toward the center of the circle (which we‚Äôll say has radius \(R\), following the direction of the force, and has its own formula and name of ‚Äúcentripetal acceleration‚Äù (\(a_c\)): \[ a_c=\frac{v^2}{R} \label{centripetala} \] From Newton‚Äôs second law, there‚Äôs then a centripetal force. That tug you provide to the ball of mass \(m\) is: \[ \begin{align} F_c &= m a_c \nonumber \\ F_c &=m \frac{v^2}{R} \label{centripetalf} \end{align} \]
